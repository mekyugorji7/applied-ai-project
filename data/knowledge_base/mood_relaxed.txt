The "relaxed" mood sits in the low-arousal, moderately positive valence quadrant of the circumplex model of affect — adjacent to chill but distinct in its greater stillness and quietude. Where chill implies relaxed alertness, relaxed implies genuine deceleration: the listener is winding down, releasing tension, and allowing the nervous system to shift toward parasympathetic dominance.

In measurable terms, relaxed tracks feature energy values in the lower range (0.28–0.50), lower even than chill in their physical intensity. Valence is moderately positive (0.55–0.78), warm and comforting without the active excitement of happy. Tempos are comfortable and unhurried (70–95 BPM), with jazz swing and smooth acoustic grooves particularly effective at this tempo range. Acousticness is typically elevated (0.65–0.92), as organic timbres are more effective at inducing genuine physiological relaxation than electronic production. Danceability sits low-to-moderate (0.40–0.60).

Musical characteristics that create genuine relaxation include slow, resolved harmonic movement, long sustain and decay tails (particularly in piano and guitar), minimal percussive attack, legato melodic phrasing, and a sense of spaciousness in the arrangement — no frequency band is overcrowded. Jazz, acoustic soul, and slow blues are particularly effective relaxed mood genres.

Context of use: evening decompression after high-stress workdays, reading background, gentle yoga or stretching, massage environments, bedtime transition from wakefulness to sleep, and quiet social settings. Relaxed mood music is physiologically functional — studies show it measurably reduces cortisol and blood pressure. It suits listeners deliberately choosing music to regulate their nervous system toward calm rather than simply seeking entertainment.
