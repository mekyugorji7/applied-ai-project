R&B (rhythm and blues) is a genre rooted in African-American musical traditions, blending gospel-influenced vocals with rhythm-section-forward production. Contemporary R&B incorporates elements of soul, funk, hip-hop, and electronic production into a smooth, emotionally resonant sound. Tempos typically range from 88 to 108 BPM, with a slow-jam tradition at 60–80 BPM and more contemporary trap-influenced R&B matching hip-hop tempos.

Energy values span low to moderate-high (0.40–0.75), reflecting the genre's range from intimate slow jams to upbeat dance-floor grooves. Acousticness is low to moderate (0.15–0.40), with modern R&B dominated by programmed beats and synthesized textures, though piano and live bass often appear. Danceability is high (0.65–0.85) — R&B groove is fundamentally body-centered. Valence is mostly positive (0.65–0.88), often romantic, hopeful, and warm.

Characteristic instruments include four-on-the-floor or hip-hop influenced drum programming, synthesizer bass or live bass, piano (acoustic or electric), Rhodes or Wurlitzer electric piano for warmth, lush string pads, and expressive lead vocals with heavy melisma, runs, and emotional inflection. Call-and-response between lead and backing vocals is a structural hallmark.

Common moods include romantic, hopeful, sensual, and euphoric. Listener use cases include date nights, intimate social settings, driving, workout playlists (for upbeat R&B), and mood-setting background for creative work. R&B is deeply associated with romantic and sexual expression — its emotional directness and vocal virtuosity make it particularly effective at evoking intimacy and connection. It suits listeners who prioritize emotional resonance, vocal artistry, and sophisticated groove-oriented production.
