Latin music encompasses a rich diversity of regional genres — salsa, reggaeton, cumbia, bossa nova, flamenco, and more — unified by rhythmic vitality, melodic warmth, and a celebratory spirit. In the context of contemporary popular Latin music, tempos typically fall between 95 and 115 BPM, with reggaeton running around 90–100 BPM and salsa pushing 180+ BPM at full speed.

Energy values are moderate to high (0.65–0.85) for most popular Latin styles. Acousticness varies widely: traditional acoustic styles (bossa nova, flamenco) run 0.50–0.80, while urban Latin (reggaeton, Latin trap) sits at 0.10–0.25. Danceability is characteristically high (0.75–0.92) — rhythm and dance are inseparable from Latin musical identity. Valence is strongly positive (0.70–0.90), reflecting the genre's romantic and celebratory emotional palette.

Characteristic instruments include percussion-forward arrangements (congas, timbales, bongos, claves, shakers), acoustic and electric guitar, piano or keyboard, brass sections, and expressive vocal performance. The clave rhythm — a two-bar pattern in 3-2 or 2-3 configuration — is the structural backbone of Afro-Cuban derived styles. Syncopation and cross-rhythmic interplay between instruments create the rich polyrhythmic texture Latin music is famous for.

Common moods include romantic, playful, joyful, and sensual. Listener use cases include dance events, dinner parties, warm-weather outdoor gatherings, cooking, and any context requiring vibrant, sociable background energy. Latin music's rhythmic complexity rewards dancers while its melodic warmth works equally well for passive enjoyment. It suits listeners who want music with infectious groove, emotional warmth, and cross-cultural appeal.
