Synthwave is a retro-futuristic electronic genre rooted in nostalgic reinterpretation of 1980s film scores, video game soundtracks, and synth-pop production. Emerging prominently in the 2010s, it celebrates analog synthesizer aesthetics, neon-lit imagery, and a cinematic sense of longing. Tempos typically range from 100 to 130 BPM, with many tracks settling around 110–120 BPM.

Energy values are moderate to high (0.60–0.85), creating a driving, immersive quality without the relentless intensity of EDM drops. Acousticness is very low (0.05–0.25) — synthwave is purely electronic in texture. Danceability is moderate-high (0.60–0.80), reflecting its danceable yet atmospheric character. Valence tends toward the bittersweet (0.40–0.65) — synthwave is emotionally complex, often evoking nostalgia, longing, and cool detachment simultaneously.

Characteristic instruments are synthesizers — particularly analog polysynths (Juno, Prophet, Oberheim), punchy TR-808 or TR-909 drum machines, arpeggiated basslines, lush chorus-drenched pads, and sax solos for the most 80s-faithful tracks. Reverb and delay are applied liberally to create spatial depth. Gated reverb on snare is an iconic production fingerprint.

Common moods include moody, nostalgic, dark, and intense. Listener use cases include night driving, late-night work sessions, gaming, and atmospheric background for creative projects. Synthwave rewards headphone listening due to its rich stereo field and layered production. It appeals to listeners who want electronic music with emotional depth and cinematic sweep — more introspective than club EDM, more propulsive than ambient.
