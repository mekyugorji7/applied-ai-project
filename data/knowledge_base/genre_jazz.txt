Jazz is a genre defined by improvisation, harmonic complexity, and rhythmic sophistication. Originating in early 20th-century New Orleans, jazz encompasses a vast stylistic range from bebop to smooth jazz. Tempos vary widely — from slow ballads at 50–70 BPM to blistering bebop at 200+ BPM — with mainstream and smooth jazz typically occupying 80–120 BPM for relaxed accessibility.

Energy values in jazz are moderate, generally between 0.30 and 0.65. Acousticness is notably high (0.70–0.95), reflecting the genre's acoustic instrumental tradition: upright bass, acoustic piano, brushed snare, and horn sections. Danceability sits around 0.45–0.65 — jazz has a groove but its rhythmic complexity (swing feel, syncopation, odd meters) differs from the four-on-the-floor accessibility of pop. Valence tends positive-neutral (0.50–0.75).

Characteristic instruments include acoustic piano, upright bass, jazz guitar, trumpet, saxophone, trombone, and a drum kit played with brushes or sticks depending on tempo. The "swing feel" — derived from triplet subdivisions of the beat — gives jazz its distinctive buoyancy. Extended harmonies (seventh chords, altered dominants, chord substitutions) create harmonic richness absent in most popular genres.

Common moods include relaxed, nostalgic, romantic, and contemplative. Listener use cases include dinner ambience, late-night unwinding, creative work environments, cocktail lounges, and focused listening by music enthusiasts. Jazz rewards attention — casual listening is pleasant, but engaged listening reveals extraordinary depth. It suits listeners who appreciate musicianship, harmonic color, and the spontaneous conversation between instruments.
