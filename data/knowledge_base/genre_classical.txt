Classical music (Western art music) encompasses a broad tradition spanning roughly 1600–1900, including Baroque, Classical, and Romantic periods, along with 20th-century extensions. Tempos vary enormously by form and era — from slow adagios at 50–65 BPM to vivace passages exceeding 168 BPM. Most concert works contain multiple tempo sections within a single composition.

Energy values span a wide range (0.15–0.80) depending on the specific work — a Beethoven symphony movement may reach 0.70–0.80, while a Chopin nocturne stays below 0.30. Acousticness is characteristically very high (0.85–0.98), as classical music is defined by acoustic instruments without electronic processing. Danceability tends low to moderate (0.25–0.55) for most orchestral and chamber works, though dance forms (Waltz, Minuet) rise higher. Valence ranges from deeply melancholic (0.15–0.30) to triumphant (0.75–0.90).

Characteristic instruments include orchestral strings (violin, viola, cello, double bass), woodwinds (flute, oboe, clarinet, bassoon), brass (horn, trumpet, trombone, tuba), concert piano, harpsichord, and timpani. Chamber music features smaller acoustic ensembles; solo piano and violin repertoire are particularly beloved.

Common moods include melancholic, triumphant, peaceful, and dramatic. Listener use cases include deep focus work (particularly Baroque counterpoint for cognitive tasks), emotional processing, intellectual engagement, and formal concert attendance. Classical music rewards repeated listening — structural complexity and thematic development yield new insights over time, making it valuable for listeners seeking intellectual and emotional depth.
