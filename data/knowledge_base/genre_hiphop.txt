Hip-hop is a rhythmically driven genre built on sampled beats, bass-heavy production, and vocal delivery that ranges from melodic singing to rapid-fire rapping. Originating in 1970s New York, hip-hop now encompasses a vast range of subgenres. Tempos typically fall between 80 and 105 BPM in mainstream and trap styles, though boom-bap may run 90–100 BPM and some variants experiment with half-time feels at 60–75 BPM.

Energy values range from 0.65 to 0.92 in most commercial hip-hop. Acousticness is low (0.05–0.20) reflecting electronic beat production. Danceability is high (0.70–0.90) — hip-hop grooves are engineered for physical response, with sub-bass kick drums and snappy snares driving movement. Valence varies widely (0.40–0.92) depending on lyrical content and production mood, from euphoric to introspective.

Characteristic instruments include sampled drum breaks (or programmed equivalents), 808 sub-bass kicks, layered synthesizer melody loops, chopped-and-pitched vocal samples, hi-hat patterns (including trap's rapid triplet hi-hats), and pitched percussion. The relationship between the beat and the vocal rhythm is the genre's core creative axis.

Common moods include euphoric, intense, confident, and introspective. Listener use cases include workouts, commuting, party playlists, and engaged listening for lyrical content. Hip-hop is deeply connected to cultural identity and social commentary, giving it emotional layers beyond pure entertainment. It suits listeners who want rhythmic momentum with verbal artistry and bass-forward production energy that commands physical attention.
