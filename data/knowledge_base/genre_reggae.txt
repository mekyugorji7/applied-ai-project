Reggae is a Jamaican genre that emerged in the late 1960s, characterized by its distinctive off-beat rhythmic emphasis and laid-back groove. The genre's defining feature is the "skank" — a choppy guitar or keyboard chord landing on the off-beats (the "and" of each beat) — creating a lilting, swaying feel unlike any other popular genre. Tempos typically range from 80 to 100 BPM, with most classic reggae at 88–95 BPM.

Energy values are moderate (0.50–0.70), capturing reggae's balance between rhythmic vitality and relaxed disposition. Acousticness is moderate (0.40–0.65), reflecting organic instrument recordings with natural room sound, though the rhythm section is always prominent. Danceability is notable (0.60–0.75), with reggae's groove designed to encourage relaxed, swaying movement rather than intense dancing. Valence is generally positive (0.65–0.85), reflecting the genre's uplifting themes.

Characteristic instruments include electric rhythm guitar playing the off-beat skank, melodic bass guitar carrying much of the harmonic and rhythmic weight (reggae bass is unusually prominent), organ or keyboard, brass sections (trumpet, trombone), and a drum kit where the snare falls on beat 3 rather than the Western convention of beat 2 or 4. This creates reggae's uniquely grounded feel.

Common moods include playful, relaxed, hopeful, and romantic. Listener use cases include beach settings, outdoor socializing, leisurely afternoon listening, and meditative states. Reggae is associated with themes of unity, spiritual consciousness, and resistance, giving it a meaningful cultural dimension beyond pure entertainment. It suits listeners who want rhythmic music with positive energy and a distinctly unhurried sense of groove.
