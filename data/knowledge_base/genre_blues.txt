Blues is an American genre born from African-American musical traditions in the Deep South, characterized by its distinctive call-and-response structure, 12-bar chord progression, and expressive guitar and vocal techniques. The blues scale — a minor pentatonic scale with an added "blue note" (flattened fifth) — gives the genre its signature sound of tension and emotional release. Tempos range from slow blues at 60–75 BPM to shuffled up-tempo blues reaching 120+ BPM.

Energy values are generally low to moderate (0.25–0.55), with slow blues representing genuine emotional restraint and up-tempo shuffles providing more kinetic energy. Acousticness varies (0.50–0.85) — acoustic delta blues runs very high, while Chicago electric blues sits lower. Danceability is moderate (0.40–0.60) with shuffle patterns providing a distinct groove. Valence tends low to neutral (0.25–0.50), reflecting blues' emotional territory of longing, loss, and resilience.

Characteristic instruments include electric or acoustic guitar (with heavy use of string bending, vibrato, and slide technique), harmonica, piano, upright or electric bass, and a drum kit with a shuffle feel. Vocal expressiveness — moaning, melisma, raw timbre — is as important as any instrument. The interplay between guitar phrasing and vocal lines is central to blues identity.

Common moods include somber, introspective, nostalgic, and cathartic. Listener use cases include late-night listening during reflective moods, emotional processing, background for creative work, and appreciation by music enthusiasts. Blues is the foundation of much Western popular music — rock, jazz, and R&B all draw heavily from its vocabulary. It suits listeners seeking emotional authenticity, technical expressiveness, and music that transforms pain into profound beauty.
