Country music is an American genre rooted in folk traditions, storytelling, and rural Americana. It combines acoustic and electric instruments in service of narrative lyrics that explore themes of love, loss, home, and working-class life. Tempos typically range from 88 to 120 BPM, with traditional country sitting around 90–105 BPM and more contemporary "bro-country" styles pushing 110–120 BPM.

Energy values are moderate (0.45–0.70), reflecting country's balance between uptempo two-step dance tracks and heartfelt ballads. Acousticness is moderate to high (0.35–0.70) depending on subgenre — traditional and bluegrass-influenced styles run acoustic, while modern country pop incorporates substantial electric production. Danceability is moderate (0.45–0.65), with dance-floor country styles running higher. Valence covers the full range from nostalgic longing (0.40–0.55) to celebratory (0.70–0.85).

Characteristic instruments include acoustic and steel guitar (including the signature pedal steel guitar for that quintessential bending tone), banjo, fiddle, dobro, acoustic bass, snare drum with a sparse rhythm, and lead vocals with a distinct twang. Harmonized backing vocals and call-and-response structures are common production elements.

Common moods include nostalgic, hopeful, romantic, and reflective. Listener use cases include road trips (especially through rural or scenic landscapes), backyard gatherings, honky-tonk dancing, and emotional storytelling engagement. Country music suits listeners who value lyrical craft and authenticity — the genre's greatest artists are celebrated as much for their writing as their performance. It rewards attentive, emotionally engaged listening.
