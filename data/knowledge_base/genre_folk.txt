Folk music is a tradition-rooted genre emphasizing acoustic instrumentation, lyrical storytelling, and communal musical heritage. Modern folk (including indie folk and contemporary singer-songwriter) merges traditional acoustic aesthetics with contemporary production sensibilities. Tempos typically span 75–110 BPM, with acoustic ballads at the lower end and uptempo traditional pieces or jigs pushing higher.

Energy values are low to moderate (0.25–0.55), consistent with folk's acoustic, intimate character. Acousticness is the highest of nearly any genre (0.75–0.95), as folk is definitionally built around acoustic guitar, acoustic bass, and organic instruments. Danceability is moderate (0.40–0.60), rising for dance forms (reels, jigs) and falling for introspective ballads. Valence covers a wide range (0.40–0.75), from melancholy longing to celebratory community.

Characteristic instruments include acoustic guitar (fingerpicked or strummed), banjo, mandolin, fiddle, hammered dulcimer, bodhran (frame drum), tin whistle, and unamplified vocals — often in close harmony. The sonic palette is warm, woody, and resonant, emphasizing natural instrument tones without heavy processing.

Common moods include dreamy, nostalgic, peaceful, and contemplative. Listener use cases include slow mornings, hiking, reading, intimate social settings, and reflective solitude. Folk music carries a sense of authenticity and human connection — it sounds like it could have been played by a friend in your living room. It suits listeners seeking emotional honesty, acoustic warmth, and the sense that music is being shared rather than performed. The genre rewards quiet attention to lyrical detail and melodic subtlety.
